## Part III: Working a Job

### 3.1 Viewing a Work Order

1. Go to **Jobs** in the navigation bar.
2. Find your work order using search, filters, or by scrolling the list.
3. Click **"View"** on the right side of the row.
4. The Work Order Detail screen shows:
 - **WO Number** and **Description**
 - **Asset** the work is being performed on
 - **Priority** and **Status** (Open, Started, Completed, Closed)
 - **Assigned Technician**
 - **Scheduled Date** and **Date Requested**
 - **Comments/Notes** section
 - **Procedure** linked to this job (if applicable)
 - **Parts Used** on this job (populated during close-out)

### 3.2 Record Locking â��⬝ One Editor at a Time

When multiple people work at the same plant, two technicians could theoretically open the same work order for editing simultaneously. Trier OS prevents this with **automatic record locking.**

**How it works:**

1. You click **Edit** on a work order.
2. The system **acquires a lock** on that work order in your name.
3. While you hold the lock, no one else can enter edit mode on the same work order.

**If someone else is already editing:**

When you click Edit and another person holds the lock, you will see an **amber banner** at the top of the work order:

> **John Smith is currently editing this work order**
> Please wait until they finish, or the lock will automatically release at 11:45 PM.

The Edit button will not activate. You can still **view** the work order in read-only mode.

**How locks are released:**

| Action | Lock Released? |
|---|---|
| Click **Save Changes** | [YES] Yes â��⬝ lock released immediately |
| Click **Cancel** | [YES] Yes â��⬝ lock released immediately |
| Click **Close** (the detail panel) | [YES] Yes â��⬝ lock released immediately |
| Browser crash or closed tab | Lock auto-expires after 5 minutes |
| Power outage or network loss | Lock auto-expires after 5 minutes |
| Editing for a long time (lock heartbeat) | Lock extends automatically every 3 minutes while you are actively editing, so it won't expire under you |

> **Important:** If a user walks away from their computer without saving or canceling, the lock automatically releases after 5 minutes. No one gets permanently locked out.

### 3.3 Updating Your Work Order Status

When you begin working on a job:

1. Open the Work Order detail (click **"View"**).
2. Click the **Edit** (pencil) button.
3. Change the **Status** dropdown from **"Open"** to **"Started"**.
4. Optionally add a **Comment** describing what you found or are doing.
5. Click **"Save Changes"**.

> **Why This Matters:** Changing status to "Started" tells your supervisor you are actively working the job. It also clears emergency notifications from the bell if this was a Priority 1.

### 3.4 Following a Procedure (Task Checklist)

If a Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) is attached to the work order:

1. Open the Work Order.
2. Look for the **"Tasks"** or **"Procedure"** section.
3. Each step is listed in order with a checkbox.
4. Check off each step as you complete it.
5. The checklist tracks your progress and saves automatically.

### 3.5 Looking Up a Part You Need

If you need a part to complete the job:

1. Click **"Parts"** in the navigation bar.
2. Type the part number, name, or description in the **Search** bar.
3. Check the **QTY On Hand** column to see if it is in stock.
4. The **Location** column tells you exactly where to find it (warehouse, aisle, bin).
5. Click the part row to see full details including vendor info, unit cost, and substitutes.

**If the part is out of stock â��⬝ see [Part V: Finding What You Need](#part-v-finding-what-you-need) for how to search other plants.**

### 3.6 Creating a New Work Order

When you discover a problem that needs its own work order:

1. Go to **Jobs**.
2. Click the green **"+ New WO"** button in the top-right corner.
3. Fill in the required fields:
 - **Asset:** Select the machine from the dropdown.
 - **Description:** Clearly describe the problem. Be specific â��⬝ future technicians and auditors will read this.
 - **Priority:** Select 1 (Emergency) through 4 (Routine).
 - **Assigned To:** Select yourself or another technician.
 - **Type:** Select the work type (Corrective, Preventive, Emergency, Inspection, etc.).
4. Click **"Save Changes"**.
5. The system assigns a unique WO Number automatically.

> **Tip:** If you need to create a work order but you are standing in front of the machine, use the **SCAN** button to scan the asset's barcode/QR tag. The system will pre-fill the asset information for you.

### 3.6 Adding Notes to a Work Order

To document your progress or leave information for the next shift:

1. Open the Work Order.
2. Scroll to the **Comments/Notes** section.
3. Type your note.
4. Click **"Add Note"** or press Enter.
5. Notes are timestamped with your username and cannot be deleted by other users.

### 3.7 Printing a Work Order

To take a paper copy to the field:

## Part IV: Closing a Job â��⬝ The Close-Out Wizard

When the work is done, closing the job properly is **critical** â��⬝ it tracks costs, deducts parts from inventory, and builds the maintenance history that drives predictive analytics.

### 4.1 Opening the Close-Out Wizard

1. Open the completed Work Order.
2. Click the **"Close Work Order"** button (green button at the bottom).
3. The **Close-Out Wizard** opens as a multi-step form.

### 4.2 Step 1 â��⬝ Labor

1. Click **"Add Labor Entry"**.
2. Select the **Employee** who performed the work (yourself or team members).
3. Enter the **Regular Hours** worked.
4. Enter **Overtime Hours** if applicable.
5. Enter **Double-Time Hours** if applicable.
6. Repeat for each person who worked on this job.

> **Why This Matters:** Labor hours feed the cost reports that management uses for budgeting. They also calculate Mean Time To Repair (MTTR) for predictive maintenance.

### 4.3 Step 2 â��⬝ Parts Used

1. Click **"Add Part"**.
2. Search for the part by number, name, or description.
3. Select the correct part from the results.
4. Enter the **Quantity Used**.
5. The unit cost auto-fills from the parts database.
6. Repeat for every part consumed.

> **Important:** Adding parts here **automatically deducts them from your plant's inventory**. This keeps stock levels accurate without separate inventory adjustments.

### 4.4 Step 3 â��⬝ Miscellaneous Costs

For external expenses not covered by labor or parts:

1. Click **"Add Misc Cost"** (if applicable).
2. Enter a description (e.g., "Crane rental", "Outside contractor", "Specialty tool purchase").
3. Enter the dollar amount.

### 4.5 Step 4 â��⬝ Downtime & Resolution

1. Enter the **Actual Downtime** (hours the machine was out of production).
2. Select the **Resolution Type** (Repaired, Replaced, Temporary Fix, No Action Needed, etc.).
3. Write a brief **Resolution Summary** describing what was done and what was found.

### 4.6 Step 5 â��⬝ Signature & Completion

1. Sign your name in the **Signature** field.
2. Review all entered data â��⬝ labor, parts, costs, and resolution.
3. Click **"Execute Close-Out"**.
4. The work order status changes to **"Closed"** and all costs are permanently recorded.

> **Once closed, a work order cannot be re-opened.** All labor, parts, and costs are locked into the financial ledger. If you made a mistake, contact your supervisor or IT admin.

## Part IV-B: Zero-Keyboard Work Order Scanner

### IV-B.1 What It Does

The **Zero-Keyboard Work Order Scanner** (`/scanner`) lets a floor technician open, update, hold, escalate, or close a work order by scanning an asset barcode and tapping a single button — no keyboard, no form-filling, no navigation required.

The server owns all workflow logic. The device only acquires the barcode and displays tap-only action buttons based on the asset's current state. This makes the system fully usable with gloves, in cold storage environments, and on Zebra TC77/TC78 hardware scanners.

**Access:** Mission Control → **Scan** tile, or navigate to `/scanner`.

### IV-B.2 Capture Modes

| Mode | How It Works |
|------|-------------|
| **Hardware wedge scanner** | Point the Zebra gun at the label and pull the trigger. The barcode lands in a hidden input and auto-submits within 80ms — no screen tap required. |
| **Camera scan** | Tap the camera icon and aim at the barcode or QR code. Supports QR, Code 128, Code 39, EAN-13, and UPC-A. |
| **Numeric fallback** | Type a short asset code manually when the label is damaged or the camera fails. |

After capture, a **1-second confirmation flash** shows the asset name and current WO state — this prevents wrong-asset errors on camera devices before committing the action.

### IV-B.3 Server Decision Logic

The server evaluates the asset state and returns a **branch code**. The device renders the correct tap-only action set for that branch:

| Branch | Condition | Tap Options |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **AUTO_CREATE_WO** | No WO exists on the asset | Automatic — confirmation screen only, WO created immediately |
| **ROUTE_TO_ACTIVE_WO / SOLO** | Active WO, you are the assigned tech | Close WO, Mark Waiting, Escalate, Continue Later |
| **ROUTE_TO_ACTIVE_WO / MULTI_TECH** | Active WO, multiple techs | Leave Work, Close for Team, Mark Waiting, Escalate, Continue Later |
| **ROUTE_TO_ACTIVE_WO / OTHER_USER** | Active WO, assigned to different tech | Join Team, Take Over, Escalate |
| **ROUTE_TO_WAITING_WO** | WO exists but is in Waiting status | Resume, Create New WO, View Status |
| **ROUTE_TO_ESCALATED_WO** | WO is Escalated | Join Response, Take Over Response, View Status |
| **AUTO_REJECT_DUPLICATE_SCAN** | Same scanId already processed | Rejected — prevents double-processing if the gun fires twice |

### IV-B.4 Hold Reasons — Tap Only, No Typing

When you tap **Mark Waiting**, a secondary picker surfaces. All choices are tap buttons — no keyboard:

| Hold Reason | PM-Exempt | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Waiting on Parts | Yes | Pauses PM compliance timer |
| Waiting on Vendor | Yes | Pauses PM compliance timer |
| Waiting on Approval | Yes | Pauses PM compliance timer |

### IV-B.5 Supervisor Review Queue

### IV-B.6 Audit Trail

Every scan event is permanently recorded in the **ScanAuditLog** table:

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| scanId | Unique identifier for this scan event |
| assetId | Asset that was scanned |
| userId | Technician who performed the scan |
| previousState | WO status before the action |
| nextState | WO status after the action |
| decisionBranch | Which branch code the server returned |
| deviceTimestamp | Time on the device at scan moment |
| serverTimestamp | Time the server processed the event |

The log is immutable. Records are never deleted.

### IV-B.7 Offline Sync

Scan events captured while offline are queued in IndexedDB on the device. When connectivity is restored, the offline-sync endpoint replays the queue in chronological order. State conflicts that emerged while disconnected are resolved automatically — the resolvedMode and conflictAutoResolved fields in ScanAuditLog record how each conflict was settled.

### Field FAQs

**Q: Can I use Trier OS on my personal phone?**
A: Yes. Trier OS is a Progressive Web App (PWA) that runs in any modern browser â��⬝ Chrome, Safari, Edge, or Firefox. Go to the server URL, log in, and optionally "Add to Home Screen" for an app-like experience. No app store download required.

**Q: What happens if I lose Wi-Fi in the middle of a work order?**
A: If you're using the PWA with offline mode enabled, your changes are saved locally in the browser's IndexedDB. When you reconnect, the system syncs your pending changes automatically. You'll see a "Pending Sync" indicator in the status bar. If you're using a desktop browser without offline mode, unsaved changes may be lost â��⬝ the Draft Manager will attempt to recover what it can on your next visit.

**Q: Can I close a work order from my phone?**
A: Yes. The full close-out wizard works on mobile. You can add labor, parts, notes, and close the WO from any device. The interface adjusts to your screen size automatically.

**Q: Why does the parts count I see differ from what's physically on the shelf?**
A: Trier OS tracks quantities based on transactions (parts used on work orders, adjustments, transfers). If someone took a part from the shelf without recording it in the system, the count will be off. The fix: perform a **physical inventory count** and use the inventory adjustment feature to correct the discrepancy. Regularly reconciling counts prevents this drift.

**Q: I found a part cheaper from a different supplier. Can I update the price?**
A: Yes. Edit the part record and update the Unit Cost. If you're part of the enterprise network, the **Network Price Discovery** feature will automatically alert sister plants if your new price is lower â��⬝ saving money across the company.

**Q: My supervisor asked for a report. Where do I find them?**
A: Reports are available under **Reports & Analytics** in the main navigation. Key reports include:
- **Enterprise Cost Comparison** â��⬝ plant vs. plant spend
- **PM Compliance** â��⬝ on-time vs. overdue percentages
- **Asset Reliability (MTBF)** â��⬝ equipment failure trends
- **Labor Utilization** â��⬝ hours by technician

Corporate users with "All Sites" access can generate reports across the entire network.

**Q: How do I transfer a part to another plant?**
A: Navigate to **Global Logistics â⬠�" Part Transfers**. Search for the part, select the sending and receiving plants, enter the quantity, and submit the transfer request. The receiving plant will see the incoming transfer in their logistics queue.

**Q: Someone else is editing the same work order I'm working on. What happens?**
A: Trier OS uses a **record lock** system. When someone opens a work order for editing, it's locked to that user for up to 5 minutes. If you try to edit a locked record, you'll see a message indicating who has it locked and when the lock expires. After 5 minutes of inactivity, the lock releases automatically.

**Q: How do I reset my password?**
A: You cannot reset your own password directly. Contact your IT administrator or plant manager â��⬝ they can reset it through the **Administration â⬠�" User Management** panel. For security, password resets are logged in the audit trail.

**Q: What does the colored dot next to a sensor mean?**
A: Sensor status indicators:
- **Green** â��⬝ Normal reading, within thresholds
- **Yellow** â��⬝ Warning â��⬝ approaching a threshold boundary
- **Red** â��⬝ Critical alarm â��⬝ reading has exceeded the threshold
- **Gray** â��⬝ No data â��⬝ sensor hasn't reported recently or is offline

### Sensor & SCADA Troubleshooting

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| **Sensor shows "No Data" (gray dot)** | The sensor hasn't reported within the expected interval. Check the PLC/SCADA connection. Verify the sensor endpoint URL is correct and that the sensor gateway is running. |
| **Sensor reads are delayed** | The default polling interval is set at the PLC level. Trier OS *receives* data â��⬝ it doesn't pull it. Check the PLC program's POST interval to `/api/sensors/reading`. |
| **False alarm on a sensor** | If you know a reading is a false alarm, you can **silence the alarm for that specific reading** from the sensor detail view. This does NOT globally disable the sensor â��⬝ only the individual triggered alarm. The alarm will re-engage on the next reading cycle. |
| **"Sensor rate limit exceeded"** | The system allows up to 1,000 sensor readings per minute per IP address. If your PLC is polling faster than this, slow down the POST interval. This limit protects the server from overload. |
| **Sensor thresholds seem wrong** | Thresholds are configured per-sensor in the Sensor Gateway settings. Check that the min/max values match the equipment specifications. Default thresholds may have been set during initial onboarding and could need adjustment for your specific equipment. |

| **Voice input isn't working on Safari (iPhone/iPad)** | Safari requires HTTPS for microphone access. Verify the URL starts with `https://`. Also check **Settings â⬠�" Safari â⬠�" Microphone** to ensure the site has permission. |

## Part XIV: Predictive Maintenance & Asset Health

### 14.1 Predictive Risk Alerts

The **Predictive Risk Alerts** card on the Dashboard automatically identifies equipment at risk of failure.

**How It Works:**
- The system analyzes your work order history to calculate **MTBF** (Mean Time Between Failures) for every asset.
- Assets with declining MTBF trends or approaching predicted failure dates are flagged.
- No configuration required â��⬝ it learns from your actual maintenance data.

### 14.2 Health Score

Every asset receives a **Health Score** from 0 to 100:

| Score | Status | Meaning |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 80â���S100 | Healthy | Normal operations, strong MTBF. |
| 60â���S79 | At Risk | Increasing repair frequency. Schedule inspection. |
| 0â���S59 | Critical | Frequent failures. Immediate engineering review required. |

**Formula:** `100 - (repairs in last 6 months Ò� 15)`, minimum of 0.

### 14.3 MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures)

MTBF represents the average number of days between unexpected failures for an asset.

- **High MTBF** (e.g., 180 days) = Reliable machine, breaks down rarely.
- **Low MTBF** (e.g., 14 days) = Unreliable machine, needs attention.

The system also tracks **MTBF Trend:**
- **Improving** â��⬝ Recent MTBF is significantly better than historical average.
- **Stable** â��⬝ Consistent with historical patterns.
- **Worsening** â��⬝ Recent MTBF is declining. Failure is becoming more frequent.

### 14.4 Predicted Failure Date

For assets with enough history, the system calculates a **Predicted Failure Date:**

`Last Repair Date + MTBF Days = Predicted Next Failure`

Assets with predicted failures within:
- **7 days** â⬠�" **High Risk** flag
- **30 days** â⬠�" **Medium Risk** flag

### 14.5 Reliability Index (ISO 14224)

| Rating | Reliability | Action |
|--------|------------|--------|
| **STABLE** (Green) | > 93% | Operating within optimal tolerances. Maintain current PM schedule. |
| **AT RISK** (Yellow) | 89â���S93% | Wear-out phase detected. Requires inspection and possible overhaul. |
| **CRITICAL** (Red) | < 89% | High probability of catastrophic failure. Immediate engineering review. |

**Formula:** `(MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR)) Ò� 100`

### 15.8 Rate Limits & Capacity Planning

Trier OS uses a **three-tier rate limit system** to keep sensor traffic and human traffic from interfering with each other:

| Traffic Type | Endpoint | Limit | What It Protects |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Login attempts** | `/api/auth/login` | 10/min per IP | Brute-force password protection |
| **PLC/Sensor data** | `/api/sensors/reading` | **1,000/min per IP** | Dedicated bucket for machine traffic |
| **All other app traffic** | `/api/*` | 200/min per IP | Normal human browsing â��⬝ work orders, parts, assets, etc. |

Sensor data and human traffic are **completely separate.** A PLC posting sensor readings at high frequency will never slow down a maintenance tech browsing work orders.

**Capacity by plant size (at the recommended 30-second polling interval):**

| Plant Size | Sensors | Posts Per Minute | % of 1,000/min Limit | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Small (single line) | 20â���S40 | 40â���S80 | 4â���S8% | [YES] Well within limits |
| Medium (2â���S3 lines) | 40â���S80 | 80â���S160 | 8â���S16% | [YES] Comfortable |
| Large (4â���S6 lines) | 80â���S150 | 160â���S300 | 16â���S30% | [YES] Plenty of headroom |
| Maximum theoretical | 250 | 500 | 50% | [YES] Still under limit |

**If the PLC exceeds the limit (1,000 posts/min from a single IP):**
The PLC gateway receives an HTTP response with this message:

```
{ "error": "Sensor rate limit exceeded (1000/min). Check PLC polling interval." }
```

**What the PLC engineer should do if they see this error:**
1. Check the polling interval â��⬝ is it set too fast (e.g., every 1 second instead of every 30 seconds)?
2. Check if a runaway script or loop is firing repeatedly.
3. If the plant genuinely needs more than 1,000 posts/min, contact IT to adjust the server configuration.

> **For PLC engineers:** The recommended polling interval is **30 seconds** for most sensors. Process-critical sensors (like pasteurizer holding tube temperature) can poll faster â��⬝ every 10 seconds is safe. There is no need to poll faster than 10 seconds for maintenance monitoring â��⬝ your SCADA system already handles real-time process control.

## Part XXII: High Availability & Server Replication

> **Added:** March 2026 â��⬝ Enables a hot standby server that stays in sync with the primary for disaster recovery.

### 22.1 What Is High Availability?

High Availability (HA) means running **two copies of the Trier OS server**:

| Server | Role | Location | Accepts Writes? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary (Master)** | Active server | On-premises (your plant network) | [YES] Yes |
| **Secondary (Replica)** | Standby server | AWS Cloud (or another location) | [NO] No (read-only) |

The Primary automatically pushes every database change to the Secondary every 60 seconds. If the Primary goes down, you promote the Secondary to Primary and continue working.

### 22.2 How Replication Works

1. **Change Capture** â��⬝ Every INSERT, UPDATE, and DELETE on the Primary is recorded in a `sync_ledger` table using SQLite triggers.
2. **Batch Push** â��⬝ Every 60 seconds, the Primary sends all unsynced ledger entries to the Secondary via a secure API call.
3. **Apply on Secondary** â��⬝ The Secondary applies the changes to its local databases, keeping them in sync.
4. **Sync Key Authentication** â��⬝ Server-to-server calls use a shared **pairing key** (not user credentials) for security.

### 22.3 Configuring HA (IT Admin)

1. Go to **Settings â⬠�" Admin Console â⬠�" Branding & Config**.
2. Scroll to the **High Availability Configuration** panel.

**On the Primary Server:**
1. Select **"Primary (Master)"** from the role selector.
2. Enter the Secondary server's address (e.g., `http://replica-server.local:3000`).
3. Click **"Generate Pairing Key"** â��⬝ a 64-character key is created.
4. Click **"Copy"** to copy the key to your clipboard.
5. Click **"Save Configuration"**.

**On the Secondary Server:**
1. Select **"Secondary (Replica)"** from the role selector.
2. Enter the Primary server's address.
3. Paste the pairing key into the **"Import Key"** field and click **"Import Key"**.
4. Click **"Save Configuration"**.

Both servers now trust each other, and replication begins automatically.

### 22.4 Monitoring Sync Status

The HA panel displays a live 6-metric dashboard:

| Metric | What It Shows |
|---|---|
| **Plants Synced** | How many of your 40+ plants are fully synced (e.g., 40/40) |
| **Pending Changes** | Number of changes waiting to be pushed to the Secondary |
| **Replication Lag** | Seconds behind â��⬝ Green (<60s), Amber (<120s), Red (>120s) |
| **Total Ledger** | Total change entries tracked in the sync system |
| **Last Sync** | Timestamp of the most recent successful sync |
| **Total DB Size** | Combined size of all plant databases |

Click the **"Test"** button next to the partner server address to check if the Secondary is reachable and see the network latency.

### 22.5 Manual Failover â��⬝ Promoting the Secondary

If the Primary server goes down (hardware failure, power outage, etc.):

1. Access the Secondary server's web interface directly (use its IP address or DNS name).
2. Go to **Settings â⬠�" Admin Console â⬠�" Branding & Config â⬠�" High Availability**.
3. Click the **"[PARTIAL] Promote to Primary"** button.
4. Enter your **admin password** to confirm. This is a safety gate â��⬝ only admins and creators can perform failover.

> **Important:** When the original Primary comes back online, it must be reconfigured as a Secondary to prevent split-brain (two servers accepting writes simultaneously).

### 22.7 Pre-Sync Snapshots & Rollback

Before the Secondary applies any replicated changes, it automatically takes a **snapshot** of the affected database. This enables recovery if replication introduces corrupt data.

- Snapshots are stored in `data/ha_snapshots/` with timestamps.
- The system keeps the **last 5 snapshots** per plant (older ones are automatically cleaned up).
- To rollback: Use the API endpoint `POST /api/ha/rollback` with the plant ID.

> **Note:** HA is an Active-Passive architecture by design. Auto-failover was intentionally NOT implemented because it risks split-brain corruption during network hiccups. Manual failover (clicking the promote button) is the safer, industry-standard approach.

### Brute Force Protection (Rate Limiting)

Trier OS includes **automatic brute force protection** on the login page. If someone (or a bot) tries to guess passwords, they will be locked out.

**How it works:**
- Each IP address is allowed **5 login attempts** per **5-minute window**
- After 5 failures, all further login attempts from that IP are blocked
- The lockout lasts for the remainder of the 5-minute window â��⬝ it does **NOT** extend with each additional attempt
- After the window expires, the counter resets and you can try again

**What the lockout looks like:**

> [PARTIAL] *"Too many login attempts. Try again in 5 minutes."*

**What to do if you get locked out:**
1. Wait 5 minutes from your first failed attempt
2. Try again â��⬝ the counter will have reset
3. If you've forgotten your password, contact your IT administrator to reset it

> **Important:** The rate limiter is per IP address. If multiple people share the same network connection (e.g., same plant WiFi), failures from one person can affect others at the same location. This is by design â��⬝ it prevents coordinated attacks from within the same network.

### Plant Data Isolation ("Plant Jail")

Trier OS enforces strict data boundaries between plants:

- **Technicians** can only see and modify data at their assigned plant(s)
- **Managers** can view data at plants where they have an explicit role
- **IT Administrators** can access all plants (required for enterprise-wide support)
- If you try to access a plant where you have no role, you'll see: *"Access Denied: You do not have an authorized role at [Plant Name]."*

This ensures that a technician at Anderson, IN cannot accidentally view or modify work orders at Tyler, TX â��⬝ even if they somehow knew the URL.

## Part XLV: AD/LDAP Directory Integration

### 45.1 Overview

Trier OS supports enterprise **Active Directory / LDAP integration** through a toggle-switch architecture. When disabled (the default), all authentication uses the local user database. When enabled, Trier OS performs **LDAP-first authentication** â��⬝ binding against your corporate directory server before falling back to local auth.

This means a single flip of the switch transitions your entire organization from local accounts to centralized directory-managed authentication, with zero downtime and zero user disruption.

### 45.2 Architecture â��⬝ How It Works

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Toggle Switch** | ON/OFF control in Admin Console. When OFF, LDAP code is never loaded. |
| **LDAP-First Auth** | Login attempts bind against AD first. If AD bind succeeds â⬠�" user is auto-provisioned locally â⬠�" JWT issued. If AD bind fails â⬠�" falls back to local password check. |
| **Protected Accounts** | The platform creator (`Doug Trier`), `trier`, and `it_admin` accounts **always** use local auth regardless of LDAP toggle. This guarantees you can never be locked out by a directory misconfiguration. |
| **Auto-Provisioning** | When a user authenticates via LDAP for the first time, their account is automatically created in the local database with the role mapped from their AD group membership. |
| **Periodic Sync** | A background cron checks LDAP config every 5 minutes. If enabled, it triggers a full user sync at the configured interval (5/15/30/60 min), creating new users, updating existing ones, and disabling removed accounts. |

### 46.12 Best Practices

1. **Start with a clear photo.** Take the photo straight-on, well-lit, with as much of the equipment visible as possible. Avoid angles that distort component positions.

2. **Name pins descriptively.** Use names that any technician would understand: "Main Drive Motor" is better than "Motor 1". Include identifiers when relevant: "Bearing #3 (Drive End)".

3. **Set health status during rounds.** Walk your route, open each Digital Twin, and update pin colors based on visual/auditory inspection. This builds a visual health history over time.

4. **Link sensors when available.** If SCADA sensors are configured, link the corresponding pin to see live data directly on the schematic â��⬝ no need to switch to the Sensor Dashboard.

5. **Upload multiple views for complex assets.** A pasteurizer, for example, might need a front photo, a P&ID, and an electrical schematic â��⬝ each with their own pins.

6. **Use the Digital Twin during shift handoffs.** Open the schematic on a large screen and point to specific pins while explaining issues to the incoming shift. Much more effective than verbal descriptions alone.

## Chapter 52: Risk Score Management for Insurance Audits

### 52.1 Overview

Trier OS generates a live **Risk Score** (0-100) for every facility and enterprise-wide. Insurance underwriters use this score -- backed by verifiable audit trails -- to assess premium eligibility and compliance posture. A higher score signals lower operational risk.

**Access:** Navigate to **Underwriter Portal** (`/underwriter`) from the Dashboard or the sidebar.

### 52.2 How the Score is Calculated

The score starts at 100 and penalties are applied for each risk factor detected at the time of calculation:

| Factor | Impact | Cap |
|---|---|---|
| Open Near-Miss Incident | -20 pts per incident | -40 pts max |
| Overdue Safety PM | -20 pts per overdue WO | -40 pts max |
| Overdue Calibration (>30 days past due) | -10 pts per instrument | -30 pts max |
| Weekly LOTO Audit completed | +5 pts | -- |
| Zero-incident week | +5 pts | -- |

Final score is clamped between **0 and 100**.

> **Example:** 2 open near-misses (-40), 1 overdue calibration (-10) = score of **50**. One completed LOTO audit that week brings it to **55**.

### 52.3 Score Caching

Risk scores are cached in memory for **60 seconds** to keep API response times under 100 ms even across large fleets. A `PUT /api/risk-scoring/:plantId` call snapshots the current score to the `RiskScoreHistory` table for long-term trend analysis.

### 52.4 Underwriter Portal

### 52.5 Risk Score Trend (Sparkline)

The scorecard header shows a 12-month sparkline sourced from `RiskScoreHistory`. The system automatically takes daily snapshots when the dashboard is loaded. Use this trend to demonstrate month-over-month improvement to your carrier.

### 52.6 Improving Your Score -- Action Checklist

1. **Clear all open Near-Miss incidents** -- even partial close-outs (status: Under Review) still count as open. Complete the investigation and set status to Closed.
2. **Complete overdue calibrations** -- any instrument >30 days past calibration due date costs 10 points. Run the Calibration module to log the service event.

## Chapter 53: Strategic Energy Arbitrage & Load Shifting

### 53.1 Overview

Trier OS reduces utility spend by intelligently timing high-load operations to coincide with **off-peak electricity pricing windows**. The **Energy Dashboard** (accessible via Utilities tab) combines real-time pricing data, asset load profiles, and a 24-hour forecast to surface actionable savings opportunities.

**Access:** Navigate to **Utilities** -> **Energy** tab, or use the "Energy Arbitrage" tile on the main Dashboard.

### 53.2 Time-of-Use (TOU) Pricing Tiers

Trier OS supports three pricing tiers:

| Tier | Description | Typical Hours |
|---|---|---|
| **Off-Peak** | Lowest rate -- best time to run high-load equipment | 9 PM - 6 AM |
| **Mid-Peak** | Intermediate rate | 6 AM - 9 AM, 6 PM - 9 PM |
| **Peak** | Highest rate -- avoid running heavy loads | 9 AM - 6 PM |

Configure your specific TOU windows under **Utilities -> Energy -> TOU Config** (`PUT /api/energy/tou-config`).

Rate multipliers set how much more expensive peak power is versus the baseline rate:
- **PeakRateMultiplier** -- e.g., `2.5` means peak costs 2.5x the base rate
- **OffPeakRateMultiplier** -- e.g., `0.6` means off-peak costs 0.6x base

### 53.3 Pricing Clock

The **Pricing Clock** is a 24-hour bar chart displayed in the Energy Dashboard that shows:
- Each hour colored by tier (green = off-peak, yellow = mid-peak, red = peak)
- A vertical marker for the current time
- A live badge showing the current tier and rate multiplier
- A **Peak Warning Banner** (amber) that appears during peak hours

This visualization allows operators to see at a glance when the pricing window will shift and plan operations accordingly.

### 53.4 Asset Load Profiles

The **Asset Loads** system tags equipment with their electrical load (kW) and a High Load flag:

| Column | Description |
|---|---|
| AssetID | Links to the Equipment/Assets table |
| LoadKw | Rated power draw in kilowatts |
| IsHighLoad | 1 = high-load asset targeted for arbitrage |
| Category | Compressor / Boiler / Refrigeration / HVAC / Other |

High-load assets are the primary targets for load-shift recommendations. Update load profiles at **Utilities -> Energy -> Asset Loads** (`PUT /api/energy/asset-loads/:id`).

### 53.5 The Arbitrage Advisor

The **Arbitrage Advisor** modal (amber button in the Energy Dashboard header) runs the 24-hour cost optimization engine:

**How it works:**
1. Fetches TOU config to build a 24-hour hourly price forecast
2. Identifies all **off-peak windows** in the next 24 hours
3. Loads all **High Load** assets for the current plant
4. For each high-load asset, calculates the **estimated savings** if the operation is shifted to the cheapest off-peak window
5. Presents per-asset recommendations with current tier, recommended delay window, and estimated savings in dollars

**Savings Formula:**
```
savings = LoadKw x operatingHours x baseRate x (peakMultiplier - offPeakMultiplier)
```

> **Example:** A 75 kW compressor during Peak (x2.5 rate) shifted to Off-Peak (x0.6 rate) saves: 75 kW x 2h x $0.12/kWh x (2.5 - 0.6) = **$34.20 per run cycle**

### 53.6 Reading the Sustainability Report

The **Print Report** button (Energy Dashboard header) generates a **Sustainability Report** via PrintEngine:

| Section | Contents |
|---|---|
| Summary | Total kWh consumed by meter type, estimated cost |
| Monthly Readings | Full table of all logged readings with meter type, value, recorded date |

Use this report for annual regulatory submissions, ESG sustainability reporting, and utility contract renegotiations.

## Part XXXIV: Offline Resilience & Plant LAN Sync

### 59.1 How LAN Sync Works

Every plant running Trier OS has a **LAN Hub** — a lightweight WebSocket server embedded in the local area network at `ws://<hub-ip>:1940`. All plant devices (Zebra scanners, tablets, supervisor workstations) connect directly to this hub without routing through the internet or the central cloud server.

When a technician scans a barcode:
1. The scan is sent to the central Express server via REST.
2. Simultaneously, the scan event is broadcast to the LAN Hub.
3. All other connected plant devices receive the update in real time — supervisor screens refresh, active WO boards update, and floor monitors reflect the new state within milliseconds.

The LAN Hub is a resilience layer, not a replacement for the central server. Central authority always wins on conflict resolution.

---

### 59.2 Offline Scan Queue

When a device loses connectivity to both the central server and the LAN Hub, scans are not lost. Each device maintains a persistent **offline scan queue** stored in IndexedDB (`TrierCMMS_Offline` / `sync_queue` store). The queue survives browser restarts, device sleeps, and network drops.

When connectivity is restored:
- The device fires an `online` event.
- The queue is drained sequentially — each queued scan is submitted to the central server and, if a hub connection is available, to the LAN Hub as well.
- Scans already marked `hub-submitted` are skipped on drain to prevent double-close.
- The queue is cleared after successful flush.

**No technician action is required.** The recovery is fully automatic.

---

### 59.3 LAN Hub Security

The LAN Hub validates every connecting device with a **JWT token** before accepting any data. Devices without a valid token are disconnected immediately. The token is derived from the same plant authentication session used for the main application — no separate credentials are required.

Expired tokens cause the device to reconnect with a fresh token after re-authentication. This prevents stale sessions from holding open hub connections indefinitely.

The hub runs on an isolated port (1940) on the plant's OT network — it is never exposed to the internet and requires no inbound firewall rules beyond the plant LAN.

---

### 59.4 Conflict Resolution

When two devices scan the same asset simultaneously — or when an offline queue flushes a scan that conflicts with a change made by another device during the outage — the system applies a deterministic resolution rule:

- **Hub wins over queue:** If a scan was already processed by the hub while a device was offline, the queued scan is discarded as a duplicate.
- **Server wins over hub:** If the central server's record contradicts the hub's broadcast state, the server state is authoritative on next sync.
- **No silent data loss:** All conflict events are logged to the plant audit trail with the reason code `OFFLINE_CONFLICT`.

---

### 59.5 Silent Auto-Close Engine

Work Segments left in `Active` state for an extended period — typically due to a missed close-out scan at end of shift — are automatically resolved by the **Silent Auto-Close Engine**, a server-side hourly cron.

The engine:
1. Queries all `Active` WorkSegments older than `autoReviewThresholdHours` (default: 12 hours; configurable per plant in `PlantScanConfig`).
2. Skips segments placed under deliberate holds (waiting-for-parts, locked-out, and other exempt reasons) — these are intentionally open.
3. Closes qualifying segments with state `TimedOut`.
4. Sets `needsReview = 1` and `reviewReason = 'SILENT_AUTO_CLOSE'` on the parent Work Order, placing it in the supervisor review queue.

**The supervisor then confirms or overrides.** Auto-close is never the final word — it is a guardrail that ensures no segment stays permanently open due to a forgotten scan.

---

### 59.6 Cache Staleness & Reconnect Behavior

Devices that have been offline for more than one cache TTL interval may hold stale asset or WO state. On reconnect:
- The client invalidates its local React Query cache for all plant-scoped data.
- A fresh fetch is issued for the current view's data before rendering resumes.
- Stale indicators are shown if the reconnect fetch is delayed by more than 3 seconds.

For supervisors, the Mission Control dashboard shows a **live device presence count** reflecting how many devices are currently connected to the plant's LAN Hub. A count of zero while the hub is expected to be running is a signal that connectivity on the plant floor should be investigated.
